What Discovery Actually Looks Like When You're Building for People Who Don't Have a Voice in Tech

User research in Addis Ababa taught me that the best product insights come before you have a framework for them. On riding minibuses instead of running interviews.

There's a version of product discovery that looks like this: recruit 8-12 participants, conduct 45-minute moderated interviews, synthesize themes, validate with a survey. It's a rigorous process. There are whole books about it. And it almost always produces insights that are correct, useful, and profoundly insufficient.

The problem isn't the methodology. The problem is the relationship between the researcher and the subject. When you're conducting a user interview, you're an outsider asking questions. The person you're talking to is, consciously or not, performing. They give you the story they think you want to hear — or at least, a curated version of the truth. The anxiety of a 7am commute that might not show up, the calculation of "if I leave now, there are two stops where I might still catch something," the learned resignation of "this is just how things work" — none of that makes it into the interview transcript.

"The best product insight is the one you observe, not the one you ask for."

What Riding the Minibus Actually Taught Me

Before I wrote a user story for Kabba, I spent weeks as a commuter in Addis Ababa. Same routes. Same hours. No clipboard, no recording device, no "I'm building a product and I'd love your feedback." Just a person trying to get somewhere, watching what happened when things went wrong.

Three things I observed that wouldn't have come from an interview:

First: the pre-departure negotiation. Every journey started with a negotiation I hadn't expected — not about price (fares were loosely standardized), but about route certainty. Before getting in, regular commuters would ask the driver specific questions: which stops would he actually commit to? Would he take the detour? Would he change routes if he spotted better fares? The negotiation wasn't hostile. It was ritualized. And it told me that reliability, not price, was the core unresolved job.

Second: the network of informal guarantees. Experienced commuters had built their own reliability systems. They knew which drivers were trustworthy. They knew which routes had backup options. They'd formed informal carpooling arrangements with coworkers. The problem wasn't that people couldn't navigate the informal system — it was that navigating it required local knowledge that newcomers and non-regulars didn't have. Reliability was available, but it wasn't a product. It was a privilege of established relationships.

Third: the emotional cost of uncertainty. The thing that didn't come through in any survey or interview I later conducted was the anxiety. Not dramatic anxiety — chronic low-grade anxiety. The background calculation running constantly: "Is this going to work today? Do I have a backup plan? How late can I afford to be?" That anxiety wasn't in any job posting or survey response. It was in people's posture, in the way they checked their phones, in the frequency with which they checked departure times that didn't exist. You don't see that in an interview room.

The Limits of Empathy as a Method

Product discovery frameworks often talk about empathy as if it's a technique — something you perform through careful listening and thoughtful questioning. I've come to believe that's a category error.

Empathy, in the sense that actually produces product insight, is lived before it's understood. You can ask a commuter how they feel about unreliable transit. You'll get an answer. But you don't know what it means until you've experienced the moment when your vehicle doesn't show up and you have to make real-time calculations about your day.

This doesn't mean structured research is useless — it's essential for validating at scale, for reaching users you can't embed with, for catching your own confirmation bias. But it means the best structured research is done after you've already developed a lived understanding of the problem. The frameworks help you see clearly. They don't create the vision.

The Discovery Sequence That Actually Works

Embed first. Observe without an agenda. Form hypotheses from observation. Then validate with structured research. In that order. Not the other way around.

Discovery in Markets Without Product Vocabulary

There's a specific challenge in building for users who have never used the category of product you're building. In Addis Ababa, we weren't replacing an existing transit app — we were introducing structured, subscription-based commuting to people who had never experienced it. Users didn't have vocabulary for what they wanted because the thing they wanted didn't exist.

This is where "what would make this better" questions fail completely. Better than what? Better than the informal minibus system is a low bar that could be cleared by almost anything. The question that mattered was: what would it mean to have this job done reliably, every day, for the rest of your working life? That question points at a completely different product.

In discovery for markets without product vocabulary, your job isn't to ask what users want. It's to understand the job deeply enough to define what they'd want if they believed it was possible. That's a different skill, and it requires embedded observation more than interview technique.

What This Changes About How I Approach Discovery Now

I approach discovery differently than I did before Kabba. A few things I do consistently:

  • I try to use the product before I talk to users about it. Not just beta-testing — actually using it for the use case it's designed for, over a sustained period. You can't ask the right questions if you haven't experienced the right problems.
  • I separate observation sessions from interview sessions. Watching someone use a product and asking them about it are different activities. Doing them simultaneously corrupts both. I now do one, then the other, then synthesize.
  • I pay more attention to what people do than what they say. When there's a gap between behavior and stated preference, behavior is always more informative. People optimize for what they actually value, not what they say they value.
  • I hold my discovery findings lightly until I've validated them at scale. The minibus observations were right — but they were one person's observations from a specific demographic perspective. Validation at scale catches the ways your embedded experience is unrepresentative.

The Part That Doesn't Show Up in Job Descriptions

There's a reason product discovery is hard to evaluate in interviews and even harder to teach: the most important parts of it aren't methodological. They're dispositional. They require genuine curiosity about people who are different from you. They require patience with ambiguity. They require the willingness to be wrong about what you thought you understood.

Riding the minibus in Addis Ababa wasn't a research technique. It was a commitment to understanding a problem on its own terms, in its own context, before imposing a framework on it. The insight that drove Kabba's product — the real job, the commitment architecture, the route-optimization focus — came from that commitment, not from a template.

The best product work I've seen starts with someone who cared enough about the problem to live with it before they tried to solve it. That's not something you can process your way to. But it's something you can choose.
