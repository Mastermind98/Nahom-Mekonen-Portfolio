How I Use Claude to Run Product Faster — Without Losing the Judgment That Actually Matters

Most PMs use AI to write faster. That's the wrong goal. The real leverage is in the quality of your thinking — and that's a different problem entirely.

There's a version of this essay that would tell you about the five AI prompts that transformed my workflow. I'm not going to write that essay. It would be dishonest, and more importantly, it would miss the point.

The honest version starts here: I was skeptical of AI tools for a long time. Not because I doubted the technology — I have a CS background and spent years building technical integrations. I was skeptical because most of what I saw PMs doing with AI was generating faster versions of output that wasn't that valuable to begin with. Faster PRDs full of fluff. Polished user stories that still missed the actual user insight. Decks that looked great and said nothing.

Speed applied to the wrong thing is still the wrong thing.

"The question isn't whether AI can write your PRD. It's whether it can help you think more clearly about what belongs in it."

What Changed My Mind

When I started using Claude seriously, I wasn't using it to write. I was using it to argue.

I'd been working on a product decision at Kabba — whether to prioritize the corporate B2B tier or double down on individual commuter retention. Both had compelling cases. Both had data behind them. I was going in circles. So I opened a Claude session and explained the situation like I was briefing a smart colleague who knew nothing about the context.

What came back wasn't a recommendation. It was three questions I hadn't asked myself. One of them — about the structural difference in purchase decision timelines between a commuter and a corporate HR director — reframed the entire analysis. The corporate buyer had a longer sales cycle but made a single decision that committed an entire company. The individual commuter decided every single morning. Those aren't two versions of the same product decision. They're two different businesses.

That's when I understood what the tool was actually for. Not output generation. Thinking acceleration.

The Workflows That Actually Move the Needle

I've used Claude consistently across product work for about a year now. The uses that have genuinely mattered fall into four categories:

1. Pre-Meeting Preparation

Before any important stakeholder conversation — an executive review, a cross-functional alignment session, a partner negotiation — I brief Claude on the context and ask it to steel-man the strongest objections I'm likely to face. Not to prepare scripted responses, but to make sure I've actually thought through the counterarguments before I'm in the room. The number of times this has surfaced a gap I hadn't considered is embarrassing in retrospect.

2. Discovery Synthesis

After a round of user interviews, I paste my raw notes and ask Claude to identify the patterns, contradictions, and missing questions. This isn't about having the AI do the analysis — it's about using it as a structured sounding board that forces me to externalize and organize what I actually heard. The act of explaining findings clearly enough for an outside reader consistently reveals gaps in my own understanding.

3. PRD First-Draft Pressure Testing

I write the PRD. Then I ask Claude to respond as a skeptical senior engineer who's been burned by underspecified requirements before. The feedback it generates is often obvious in retrospect — but obvious-in-retrospect is exactly the category of issue that causes return trips for clarification. Better to find them before the sprint kicks off.

4. Decision Documentation

When I make a significant product call, I use Claude to help me write a clean decision record: what we decided, what we considered, what data we had, what we're trading off. Not for the record — for my own thinking clarity. If I can't explain a decision clearly enough for a smart outsider to understand the reasoning, I haven't thought it through well enough to make it yet.

What It Can't Do (And Shouldn't)

Here's what I've learned not to outsource:

Judgment about what matters. Claude can help me analyze a set of options. It cannot tell me which one matters more, because that answer is embedded in organizational context, relationship dynamics, and strategic priorities that live entirely in my head. The moment you let AI make prioritization decisions for you, you've given up the most important part of the job.

User insight from lived experience. The insight that drove Kabba's commitment architecture — that commuters needed predictability, not just reliability, and that a booking fee was actually a trust mechanism — didn't come from analysis. It came from riding a minibus at 7am and watching what happened when one didn't show up. No language model replaces the physical experience of being a user of your own product.

Stakeholder relationships. The trust that makes cross-functional work possible is built in conversations, over time, through demonstrated judgment and follow-through. AI can help you prepare for those conversations. It cannot have them for you.

For Your Team — Not Just Yourself

The most underused application of Claude in PM work isn't personal productivity. It's team leverage.

Think about the amount of time your team spends on low-leverage writing: meeting summaries, status updates, stakeholder briefings, documentation of decisions everyone already knows were made. Every hour spent on that is an hour not spent on discovery, analysis, or the kind of thinking that actually advances the product.

Here's how I've used Claude to give that time back:

  • Sprint retrospective synthesis. Feed the raw notes from a retro into Claude and ask it to surface patterns across the four quadrants. Turns a 45-minute synthesis exercise into a 10-minute review.
  • Async decision alignment. When a decision needs to go to multiple stakeholders for input, have Claude draft the decision brief. The draft will be ~70% right. What matters is that everyone responds to the same framing — it removes the interpretation variance that kills async alignment.
  • Onboarding new team members. Building a context document for a new engineer or designer? Ask Claude to structure the information you already have. You'll still need to fill in the substance — but the structure you'd otherwise spend hours on is done in minutes.
  • Spec review before engineering handoff. Have Claude review a spec as a critical engineer before it goes to the actual engineers. It catches the category of obvious gaps faster than you will after writing the document yourself.

The pattern across all of these: use AI for the structured work so humans can focus on the judgment work. The goal is never to automate product management. The goal is to give the people doing it more time to do it well.

The Real Leverage

I've been thinking about this through the lens of what makes a good PM genuinely hard to replace. It's not writing ability. It's not even analytical skill. It's the capacity to hold context across a complex product and organizational landscape, make judgment calls under uncertainty, and move a diverse set of stakeholders toward a shared direction.

AI makes you faster at the parts that aren't those things. Which means — if you use it right — it gives you more time and cognitive bandwidth for the parts that are.

That's the leverage. Not a faster PRD. More space to think clearly about the problem the PRD is trying to solve.

"Give me six hours to chop down a tree and I will spend the first four sharpening the axe." The tool doesn't do the thinking. It buys you time to do it better.

Start with one workflow. Not five. Pick the one where your current process has the most waste, and build from there. The PMs who will compound the most from these tools are the ones who use them to raise the floor on their own thinking — not the ones chasing the fastest output.
