Case study: Kabba for Business
B2B corporate tier · the deep dive
CS-01
role: Senior PM, product owner
shipped: 2023, live
team: 6 eng · ops · finance
outcome: core revenue line

How I turned employee commuting into Kabba's highest margin product. The full arc, the way I would walk a hiring manager through it: the problem, the research, the options I killed, what shipped, and what moved.

# 1 · the problem

I kept seeing the same pattern in our ridership data: clusters of riders leaving the same office parks at the same time, many reimbursed by their employer. Digging in, I found companies were quietly running their own staff transport, renting buses, hiring drivers, and eating the safety complaints and no-shows themselves. It was expensive, off-mission, and a liability nobody wanted to own.

So I went and asked. I sat with 30 HR and operations leaders across 20 companies. The line that reframed the product came from an HR director who told me, plainly: "I don't want to be in the transportation business. I want my people to show up on time and I want the risk off my desk." They were not buying vehicles. They were buying attendance and peace of mind.

They weren't buying transportation. They were buying attendance, and the liability off their desk.
# 2 · why it mattered to the business

B2C revenue swung with weather, fuel, and paydays. Corporate contracts were the opposite: recurring, predictable, and high margin, and every company that signed anchored a route with guaranteed daily riders, which made the B2C service on that route more reliable too. One sale improved two products. That is the kind of leverage worth chasing.

# 3 · the options, and the ones I cut
Decision record
chose
A subscription tier with guaranteed seats on the shared network: a company admin dashboard, per-employee monthly billing, and attendance reporting for HR.
over A
Per-ride corporate billing. Cut it: no budget predictability for HR, and it guaranteed no capacity at peak, the exact thing they were paying to stop worrying about.
over B
Dedicated white-label shuttles per client. Cut it: capital heavy, low margin, and it threw away the shared network that made us different from the bus rental they already hated.
the bet
Guaranteed seats on shared routes gave HR a predictable line item and gave us margin without buying a single new bus. I used RICE to sequence: admin dashboard and billing were must-haves, advanced analytics waited.
# 4 · what we shipped

I wrote the specs and worked daily with six engineers, the operations team on route capacity, and finance on billing, and I presented the tier directly to client HR leaders and to our own executive team. We shipped in three parts:

  • An HR admin dashboard to enroll employees, assign them to routes, and see who is riding, the visibility that replaced their spreadsheets and phone calls.
  • Guaranteed seats on fixed routes with monthly per-employee billing, so HR got one predictable invoice instead of managing a fleet.
  • Attendance and usage reporting that gave HR proof their people were getting to work, the outcome they actually cared about.
# 5 · the impact

Piloted with one anchor client of about 200 employees, then rolled out over roughly 18 months to a core book of business.

Reflection · what I would change
I found this too late. The demand was sitting in our ridership data from early on. I ran B2B discovery in month 18, not month 2. Our highest margin product should have been a hypothesis I tested in the first quarter, not a pattern I stumbled into.
I under-instrumented the pilot. I had strong qualitative signal from HR but thin baseline numbers, so the before/after took longer to prove than it should have. Now I set the measurement before the pilot, not after.
Case study: LinQ
Student transportation · built solo, free
CS-02
role: solo, on weekends
shipped: live
cost to operators: free
market: Minnesota, North Carolina

A side project I could not put down, and proof I can take a product from a real problem to a shipped, adopted tool on my own. Small operators move thousands of kids in seven-seater vans on paper and phone calls. I built them the software the big yellow-bus vendors never bothered to.

# 1 · the problem

"Type 3" operators run seven-seater vans for school districts, and they are invisible to the software market: too small for the big yellow-bus vendors to bother with. So they run routes on spreadsheets, sticky notes, and phone calls. And parents have zero visibility, no idea where their child is between home and school. I saw it up close, operators in my own community, family included, drowning every morning.

# 2 · the research

I sat with about 12 operators and rode along on the morning run. The real job was not the "route optimization" the big tools sell. It was two smaller, sharper things: the dispatcher knowing exactly who is on which van right now, and the parent knowing their kid is safe. Everything else was noise on the one morning that mattered.

Parents called the office all morning asking one question: where is my kid? Nobody could answer it.
# 3 · the options, and the one I cut
Decision record
chose
Build only two things: the dispatcher's daily screen and a live map for parents.
over
Matching the big vendors' feature lists, billing, invoicing, compliance reporting.
the constraint
I had weekends and nothing else, so I cut everything that was not the job the morning revolved around. Operators adopted it anyway, because it did the one thing paper could not.
# 4 · what shipped
  • A dispatcher daily screen with drivers, routes, and student assignments on one view, replacing the spreadsheet and the sticky notes.
  • A live parent app showing the real-time location of the van carrying their child, the feature I cared about most.
  • Routing and dispatch for seven-seater fleets, the segment the big-bus software had ignored.

Reflection · what I would change
Free got adoption but capped the learning. Giving it away got operators in fast, but without a price signal I could not tell which features they truly valued. Next time I would charge a small amount early, not for money, but for the truth.
I built for the operators I could reach. Mostly my own network. I would run wider discovery before assuming their morning was every operator's morning.

2.0How I work

Four habits under everything above. The domain keeps changing. These do not.

  • Live the problem before I build. Two weeks as a user beats two months guessing. I rode the minibuses and I sat with the dispatchers.
  • Find the real job. People rarely want the feature they name. They want the outcome underneath it, and that is what I design toward.
  • Ship small and let reality vote. A rough version in someone's hands teaches me more than a flawless spec ever has.
  • Measure what changes a decision. If a number will not change what we do next, I leave it alone.
