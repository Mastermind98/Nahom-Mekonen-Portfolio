Reliable transportation quietly decides where people can work, what they can earn, and how a city grows. Once I saw that up close, I could not unsee it. It is the problem I most want to keep working on.

1.0Background

I came to the US from Ethiopia to study computer science. I thought I would become a software engineer, but somewhere in college growth marketing pulled me in, and I started building small direct to consumer brands out of my dorm.

That turned into Radium Media. Over three years it grew to $30M in sales with no outside money, and it taught me more than any class did. When I graduated I had offers from big companies, and I am grateful I got them, but there was a problem back home I could not stop thinking about.

So I went back to Ethiopia. The countries growing fastest in the world run on transportation that was built decades ago, when it was built at all. Getting that right changes how millions of people live and work every day. I wanted to spend my energy there.

That became Kabba, where we are building public transit where there was almost none. Before I wrote a single requirement, I rode the minibuses for weeks as a commuter, because I did not want to guess at a problem I could go and live instead. Later I built LinQ for the same reason, since the same struggle follows kids to school, here and back home.

2.0Thesis

I choose to work in emerging markets because that is where the need is greatest and, honestly, where the work feels most worth doing. These economies are growing fast on transport systems that have not kept up, and closing that gap matters to real people every single day.

It is also genuinely hard, and I have a lot of respect for how hard. A few things I had to learn on the ground:

  • Cash economies with little card use, so payments were something we had to design from scratch, not a service to plug in.
  • Regulation that runs through government, where every launch started as a conversation, not a release.
  • Low digital trust, where we earned adoption slowly, one honest interaction at a time.
  • Infrastructure I could not assume, from maps to power to the roads themselves.

Building through all of that changed how I think about product. It made me more patient, more careful about what actually matters, and a lot more grateful when something finally works.

Three things I have built. For each, the problem, the calls we made, what happened, and what I would do differently.

Kabba Transport
Series A mass transit · Addis Ababa
KB-021
status: shipped, live
role: co-founder & head of product
timeline: 2021 to present
surface: rider · driver · operator

Kabba is the company I care about most. We are building shared mass transit for Addis Ababa, a city where about five million people move every day with no real system built for them. Not ride hailing, not a taxi app. Actual commuter infrastructure: fixed routes, subscriptions, predictable pricing.

# the problem

Before I wrote a single requirement, I rode the informal minibuses for weeks, as someone living the problem rather than studying it. The insight that reorganized everything for me: the job is not "get a ride." It is "start work on time, every day, without wondering whether I will make it." Once I saw the job that way, the product decisions started to line up behind it.

Ride hailing had already arrived. It solved reliability, then priced itself out of the working professional's day. The informal minibuses had the opposite problem. Affordable, but no structure, no predictability, no trust. Nobody had built the thing in between, and that is what we set out to do.

# how I worked

I owned the product end to end, from discovery to specs my engineers could ship without coming back for clarification. I was lucky to work with a 10-engineer team, and together we built for 40+ enterprise clients and 30 school partners.

  • Led product strategy and roadmap across rider, driver, and operator surfaces. Used RICE scoring to force sequencing when engineering bandwidth and operational urgency collided.
  • Interviewed more than 100 companies and ran discovery with commuters and drivers. That work surfaced that a driver's real job was predictable income, not navigation, and I rebuilt the driver app around it.
  • Mapped the operator workflow end to end. Financial reconciliation was the quiet bottleneck, invisible from outside but fatal to scaling, so it became the top backlog item before any fleet expansion.
  • Specified and owned the payment gateway and government API integrations, managing external technical and compliance stakeholders from brief to go live.
  • Built the B2B corporate tier from user stories up. HR directors did not want transportation. They wanted liability off their desk, and that reframe made it our highest margin product.
  • Built the analytics layer behind real time operating decisions and used it to test routes and pricing before committing to full rollout.
Retro · open questions
Instrument earlier, decide later. I was calling product decisions directionally well after we had the usage to be precise. Directional is fine before product market fit. After it, that is just an excuse.
Run B2B discovery in month 2, not month 18. Corporate HR turned out to be our most rational, highest value buyer. Running that discovery earlier would have reshaped the roadmap.
Make the driver feedback loop a standing channel. Driver sessions kept handing me critical insight. I treated them as lucky finds. They should have been a fixed input from day one.

type: personal project
status: shipped, live

LinQ is a side project I built because the problem kept bugging me. School buses have plenty of software. The seven-seater vans that quietly move thousands of kids every day have almost none. So I built the tool they were missing: routing, dispatch, and live tracking for the small operators that districts actually contract.

# the build
  • Gave dispatchers one screen to manage drivers, routes, and student assignments, the kind of visibility big-bus software never bothered to build for type 3 fleets.
  • Put a live map in parents' hands, so they can see where their child is on the way to school. That was the part I cared about most.
  • Routed 10,000+ students for 10+ operators across Minnesota and North Carolina, turning spreadsheets and phone calls into a real system.

The part that stays with me is simple. We can track a package across an ocean, and I did not want a child to be any harder to find on the way to school.

Where it started. A creator commerce company I built and ran on one bet: creator audiences are the most loyal, high intent buyers in e-commerce, and almost nobody was building brands for them on purpose.

# the insight

Most companies saw a creator audience as ad inventory. I read it as a product brief. A mid tier creator with 200,000 engaged followers holds the one thing brands burn millions trying to fake: trust. So I built brands backwards. Audience first, product second, with the creator as proof the market already existed.

  • Built the audience persona before the product, down to how they buy and what earns their trust.
  • Validated fit before spending, A/B testing the message with each audience before scaling a dollar.
  • Turned it into a playbook that worked on the fifth brand as well as the first.
Retro · what I learned
Build retention from day one. I optimized hard for acquisition. The brands that lasted had a real repeat purchase loop, and I learned that a few brands too late.

6.0Non-goals

A spec is honest about what it is not. So I will be too.

  • I am not leaving Kabba because it failed. It works, and I am proud of it. I want my next chapter to be hands on, inside a mission I can really pour into.
  • I am not looking to start another company right now. I have loved building from zero, but what I want next is to go deep on one product with a team I can learn from.
  • I am not chasing a title. I would rather own a real problem than sit higher on an org chart.
  • I try not to track numbers that do not change a decision. I would rather measure less and actually act on it.
